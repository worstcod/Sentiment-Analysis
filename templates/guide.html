📖 User & Operations Guide

Comprehensive guide to navigating, understanding, and utilizing the Macro-Economic Sentiment Terminal.

1. Quick Start Guide

The terminal correlates historical price movements of major financial assets with breaking geopolitical and macroeconomic news events. To run a query:

  1. Select Asset Class: Choose between Stocks, Cryptocurrencies, Indices, Commodities, Forex, or Multi-Asset Comparisons.
  2. Select Preset/Custom Ticker: Select a pre-configured ticker or enter a manual ticker symbol. In Custom mode, you can compare multiple symbols by separating them with commas (e.g., AAPL,MSFT,NVDA).
  3. Event Keyword Search: Enter the news keyword to align with price history. You can search for multiple keywords by separating them with a plus sign (+), which searches Google News using an OR operator logic (e.g. Inflation+Interest Rate matches stories containing either term).
  4. Set Date Range: Choose start and end boundaries (it is recommended to use a window of 30 to 90 days for optimal readability).
  5. Click Generate Analysis to fetch market indices, download feed entries, run the FinBERT sentiment model, and map markers.

2. Quantitative Metrics & Math (In-Depth)

Annualized Volatility (Standard Deviation)

What it is: Volatility measures the spread or dispersion of an asset's daily price swings. A higher volatility means the price undergoes larger and more frequent spikes or drops. To make volatility comparable across different timelines, we convert the daily return dispersion into annual terms.

The Math: The daily return standard deviation is scaled by the square root of the asset class's standard annual trading session count ($N$):

σannualized = σdaily × √N

Where $N = 365$ for Cryptocurrencies (trade 24/7), $N = 260$ for Forex pairs (trade 5 days/week plus overnight), and $N = 252$ for Stocks/Commodities.

Example: If a stock (`AAPL`, $N=252$) has a daily standard deviation of $1.20\%$, its annualized volatility is: 1.20% × √252 = 1.20% × 15.8745 = 19.05%

Maximum Drawdown

What it is: Maximum Drawdown measures the largest cumulative drop in the asset's price from its peak value to its lowest trough during the selected timeline. It represents the "worst-case scenario" loss you would have experienced if you bought at the absolute peak and sold at the absolute bottom.

The Math: At each day $t$, we compute the percentage distance from the highest price observed up to that point ($RollingMax$):

Drawdown(t) = (Price(t) - RollingMax(t)) / RollingMax(t)

The minimum value recorded across the entire daily series represents the Maximum Drawdown (the deepest drop).

Example: If a stock's price moves as `[100, 120, 110, 90, 130]`: - Rolling Peak = [100, 120, 120, 120, 130]
- Drawdown series = [0%, 0%, -8.33%, -25.0%, 0%]
- Max Drawdown = -25.0% (at day 4 price of 90)

Indexed Standardization (Rebased to 100)

What it is: If comparing Apple (priced at \$180) and Bitcoin (priced at \$65,000) on the same chart, the scale difference makes direct visual comparison impossible. To fix this, we rebase both prices to start at exactly 100 on the first day of the selected period.

The Math: Every daily price is divided by the first day's closing price and multiplied by 100:

Priceindexed(t) = (Price(t) / Price(Tfirst)) × 100

Example: If Bitcoin is \$60,000 on day 1 and rises to \$63,000 on day 5: Rebased Price = ($63,000 / $60,000) × 100 = 105.00 This indicates a net 5% gain since the beginning of the period.

Pearson Daily Return Correlation Matrix

What it is: In Multi-Asset Comparison mode, the terminal computes a daily returns correlation matrix to show how closely the assets moved together. Coefficients range from -1.00 (perfect opposite moves) to +1.00 (perfect parallel moves), with 0.00 representing completely independent moves.

Annualized Sharpe Ratio

What it is: The Sharpe Ratio measures "risk-adjusted return". It answers the question: Is this asset's return worth the price swings (volatility) you had to endure, or could you have made a safer return elsewhere?

The Math: Annualized returns are calculated by multiplying the daily average return by the session count ($N$), and standard deviations are annualized by multiplying daily returns standard deviation by $\sqrt{N}$. We assume a default risk-free rate ($R_f$) of $4.0\%$ ($0.04$):

Sharpe = (Mean(Rdaily) × N - Rf) / (Std(Rdaily) × √N)

How to interpret the Sharpe Ratio:

  • < 0.0: Bad (you underperformed safe US government bonds).
  • 0.0 - 1.0: Suboptimal to acceptable risk-adjusted return.
  • > 1.0: Good (high return relative to price swings).
  • > 2.0: Excellent.
  • > 3.0: Outstanding/Elite.

Annualized Sortino Ratio

What it is: Similar to the Sharpe Ratio, the Sortino Ratio measures the strategy's risk-adjusted return, but it **only penalizes downside risk** (negative returns). It ignores positive volatility because price jumps represent profit runs, not capital danger.

The Math: The annualized strategy return ($R^{strat}_a = \bar{R}^{strat} \times N$) net of risk-free rate ($R_f = 4\%$) is divided by the annualized downside volatility ($\sigma_d = s_d \times \sqrt{N}$), which is the standard deviation computed strictly over negative excess returns:

Sortino = (Rstrata - Rf) / σd  •  σd = Std(Min(0, Rstratdaily - Rf/N)) × √N

Sortino Scale Interpretation:

  • < 0.0: Bad (underperformed treasury bills).
  • 0.0 - 1.0: Standard risk-adjusted return profile.
  • > 1.0: Good downside risk-adjusted return.
  • > 2.0: Excellent downside risk-adjusted efficiency.

Strategy Calmar Ratio

What it is: The Calmar Ratio evaluates the strategy's return relative to its worst drawdown risk. It measures the relationship between potential reward and worst-case scenario pain.

The Math: The annualized strategy return ($R^{strat}_a$) divided by the absolute maximum strategy peak-to-trough drawdown ($MDD^{strat}$):

Calmar = Rstrata / MDDstrat

Calmar Scale Interpretation:

  • < 0.0: Strategy lost money over the period.
  • 0.0 - 1.0: Suboptimal return relative to drawdown size.
  • > 1.0: Good (annual return exceeds worst drawdown drop).
  • > 2.0: Outstanding risk efficiency (highly sought after by macro hedge funds).

Systematic Risk (Market Beta)

What it is: Beta ($\beta$) measures how much an asset's price moves when the broader market benchmark moves. It tells you if the asset acts as a high-sensitivity market amplifier or a defensive hedge.

The Math: Beta is the covariance of the asset's daily returns with the market benchmark's returns, divided by the variance of the market returns:

β = Covariance(Rasset, Rmarket) / Variance(Rmarket)

Benchmark Configuration:

  • Single Ticker Mode: Benchmark defaults to the S&P 500 (`^GSPC`) or Bitcoin (`BTC-USD`) if the ticker is a cryptocurrency.
  • Comparison Mode: Benchmark defaults to the first (primary) ticker entered in the basket, evaluating how other tickers fluctuate relative to it.

How to interpret Beta:

  • β = 1.0: Moves in lockstep with the market benchmark.
  • β > 1.0: High sensitivity (e.g. if market rises 10%, asset rises 15%; if market drops 10%, asset drops 15%).
  • β < 1.0: Defensive (e.g. utilities, gold; less sensitive to market shocks).
  • β < 0.0: Moves in the opposite direction of the market benchmark.

News Sentiment Lead-Lag Analysis

What it is: This analysis answers the ultimate trading question: Does news sentiment predict the price, or is price movement driving the news stories?

The Math: We shift the daily sentiment series by lag $k$ (from -3 to +3 days) and compute its Pearson correlation with the daily asset returns series:

Lead-Lag Corr(k) = Correlation(Sentiment(t - k), Returns(t))

How to read the Bar Chart:

  • Positive Lags (+1d, +2d, +3d): Correlates past sentiment with today's returns. If these bars are tall and positive, **News Sentiment LEADS (predicts) Price**.
  • Lag 0d (Same-Day): Shows contemporaneous same-day reaction.
  • Negative Lags (-1d, -2d, -3d): Correlates future sentiment with today's returns. If these bars are tall, **Price LEADS News** (news reports are just reacting to previous price trends).

Event Study & Cumulative Abnormal Returns (CAR)

What it is: Isolates the occurrence of specific news articles (events) and aggregates the price behavior of the asset in the trading days surrounding the announcement. By using a benchmark market index, we filter out systematic market movements to compute the pure abnormal response triggered by the news.

The Math: For each news event $i$, we compute the daily Abnormal Return ($AR_{it}$) by running an OLS linear regression of the asset's returns against a market benchmark (S&P 500 or Bitcoin) over the full history to estimate systematic risk coefficients ($\alpha_i, \beta_i$):

ARit = Rit - (αi + βi × Rmt)

We accumulate these abnormal returns across the event window offsets $\tau \in \{-10, \dots, +10\}$, rebasing day 0 to exactly 0%:

CARi(τ) = Σ ARi, t+j × 100

Statistical Significance at t = +5 Days: To verify if the post-event drift is genuine or statistical noise, the terminal calculates a two-tailed p-value at day +5. It computes the sample standard deviation ($s$) of day +5 returns across all events, derives the Standard Error ($SE = s / \sqrt{n}$), and evaluates the t-statistic ($t = \overline{CAR}(5) / SE$) using a high-precision logistic approximation of the standard normal CDF ($\Phi$):

p-value = 2 × (1 - Φ(|t|))  •  Φ(|t|) ≈ 1 / (1 + e-y) where y = |t| × (1.5976 + 0.07056 × |t|2)

If the resulting p-value is less than $0.05$ ($5\%$), the cumulative abnormal response is statistically significant (we reject the null hypothesis of no event effect), flagged with an asterisk (`*`) in the chart legend.

Friction-Adjusted Sentiment Strategy Backtester

What it is: Simulates the performance of an algorithmic trading model that systematically trades the asset based on incoming news sentiment, incorporating realistic transaction costs to ensure professional-grade accuracy.

Trading Rules:

  • Go Long (+1): Enters a long position if the daily average news sentiment is greater than $+0.15$.
  • Go Short (-1): Enters a short position if the daily average news sentiment is less than $-0.15$.
  • Causality Lag: Signals are lagged by 1 trading day ($Signal_{t-1}$) relative to asset returns ($R_t$). This ensures you only trade at the close *after* news sentiment becomes public, preventing look-ahead bias.
  • Time-Decay Liquidation: If no news matching the keyword is published for 5 consecutive trading days, the position automatically decays and liquidates to cash ($0$) to mitigate holding risk.
  • Transaction Friction Model (0.15%): Applies a 0.15% fee (covering 0.10% broker commissions and 0.05% bid-ask slippage spread) on position turnover:
    Frictiont = |Signalt-1 - Signalt-2| × 0.0015
    For example, shifting from Long ($+1$) to Short ($-1$) represents a turnover of 2 units, incurring a $2 \times 0.15\% = 0.30\%$ transaction drag.

3. Dashboard Workspace Tabs & Legend

The terminal separates core visualizations and advanced quantitative calculations into two dedicated workspaces:

1. 📊 Market Overview Tab: Loads the core asset price overlay chart, the 20-day Simple Moving Average (dashed gold line), news sentiment triangles (green for bullish, red for bearish, gray for neutral), trading volume subplot (buying volume in green, selling volume in red), the full contextual news list, and the daily returns correlation matrix in comparison mode.

2. ⚡ Pro Quant Terminal Tab: Loads risk-adjusted quantitative metrics cards (Sharpe Ratio, Market Beta, and cumulative strategy return percentage) along with three advanced Plotly charts (Lead-Lag correlation bar chart, Event Study trajectory lines, and the Backtest simulation equity curves).

Price Chart Legend

  • Cyan Line: Daily closing price.
  • Gold Dashed Line: 20-Day Simple Moving Average (SMA-20) representing support levels.
  • Green Triangles: Bullish news event overlays.
  • Red Triangles: Bearish news event overlays.
  • Gray Circles: Neutral news event overlays.

Volume Subplot

  • Green Bars: Days where price closed higher than the previous day.
  • Red Bars: Days where price closed lower than the previous day.
  • Height represents total trading volume, illustrating buying pressure or panic selling.

4. Technical Integrity & Causality Controls

To guarantee academic-grade scientific accuracy and prevent look-ahead bias, the terminal enforces a Causality Control System:

Backward-Fill news date mappings: In traditional models, weekend news is often aligned to Friday's price. This violates causality because Friday's close occurred before Saturday's news broke. The terminal maps weekend and overnight stories to the close of the next available trading session (Monday's close).

LRU Cache Architecture: Multi-worker environments cache calls to Yahoo Finance and Google News on a per-ticker and per-date basis, preventing external server throttling and keeping processing lag below 50ms.
